How Computers See Molecules: SMILES, InChI, and Molecular Representations

\n

The Challenge: Humans Understand Molecules, Computers Do Not

\n

Chemists have drawn millions of molecular structures over the last century. We can recognize many of them instantly. A computer, however, cannot tell whether a drawing represents ethanol, aspirin, or caffeine. A computer sees a molecular drawing as a collection of pixels. It does not automatically understand atoms, bonds, functional groups, or isomerism.

\n

If computers cannot understand molecular drawings, how can they search databases containing millions of compounds or build AI models that predict chemical properties? More fundamentally, how are molecules stored in a computer at all?

\n

Molecular Representations: The Digital Language of Chemistry

\n

The solution is molecular representations. Molecular representations are ways of describing molecules in forms that computers can understand. They enable chemists to store molecules in a database, search and retrieve them not as images but as a machine-readable representation.

\n

Instead of seeing molecules as drawings, computers represent them as text strings, connection tables, graphs, or numerical features. These representations form the foundation of modern cheminformatics. Without them, chemical databases, QSAR models, and AI-driven chemistry would not exist.

\n

SMILES: A Text Language for Molecules

\n

One of the simplest ways computers represent molecules is through text. Because computers prefer text rather than drawings, chemists developed line notations to encode molecular structures. The most widely used molecular text representation is SMILES (Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System). SMILES represents chemical structures compactly as text strings using a simple set of rules:

\n

Rule 1

\n

Carbon → C

\n

Oxygen → O

\n

Nitrogen → N

\n

Atoms are represented by their atomic symbols.

\n

Rule 2

\n

Simple hydrogen atom connections are omitted.

\n

Rule 3

\n

Neighboring atoms stand next to each other.

\n

Rule 4

\n

In SMILES, double and triple bonds are represented by “=” and “#”, respectively.

\n

Rule 5

\n

Branches are represented by parenthesis. The atom inside the parentheses branches from the preceding atom.

\n

Rule 6

\n

Rings are represented by assigning digits to the two “connecting” ring atoms

\n

In SMILES, hydrogen atoms attached to common organic atoms such as carbon are usually implied rather than written explicitly.

\n

Why has SMILES become the dominant molecular representation?

\n

SMILES is simple, compact, and easy for both humans and computers to use. For example, a single line of text can represent an entire molecule, making it easy to store millions of compounds efficiently in databases. Researchers can store, share, and analyze molecular information across different databases and software tools. This widespread compatibility has made SMILES the default input format for AI applications and many cheminformatics tools, including RDKit and OpenBabel.

\n

Is SMILES Perfect?

\n

SMILES notation is easy for humans and computers to understand and use; however, it lacks uniqueness. A molecule can have more than one SMILES string. For example, both CC(=O)O and CC(O)=O are valid SMILES representations of ethanoic acid.

\n

Different SMILES representations of the same molecule can create duplicate entries in chemical databases, leading to inconsistencies in data management and potentially affecting machine learning workflows if the data are not standardized.

\n

InChI: A Universal Chemical Identifier

\n

By providing a string of characters that uniquely represents a chemical structure, InChI (International Chemical Identifier) solves the uniqueness issue found with SMILES. InChI is a standardized identifier that enables unambiguous identification of molecules across databases and software platforms. The same molecule always produces the same InChI identifier, regardless of who generates it or where it is stored.

\n

\n
